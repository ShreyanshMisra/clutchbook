## 1. What is a Delaware C-Corp? (The "Startup Default")

A Delaware C-Corporation is a specific legal structure that treats our business as an entirely separate legal "person" from the three of us. It has its own bank accounts, signs its own contracts, and pays its own taxes.

It involves two distinct components:

1. **The Geography (Delaware):** We are physically located in Massachusetts, but we will legally birth the company in Delaware. Delaware has a specialized court (*The Court of Chancery*) dedicated entirely to business law with expert judges and no juries. Because of this, it has the most predictable, business-friendly laws in the world.
2. **The Tax Class (C-Corp):** The IRS taxes us under "Subchapter C." This allows us to issue stock, create an employee option pool, and have an unlimited number of investors or share classes.

## 2. Why this is the Right Choice for Our Overlay Platform

### We Cannot Raise Investment Capital with an LLC

Our app sits at the intersection of gaming, fintech, and legal compliance. We are going to need capital to scale our user acquisition and fund our data pipelines. **Venture Capitalists (VCs) and angel investors will not invest in an LLC.** LLCs pass their profits and losses directly onto the owners' personal tax returns via a form called a Schedule K-1. Investors do not want our company’s tax liabilities hitting their personal returns. They expect a Delaware C-Corp, period.

### Protecting the Company's Code and Intellectual Property (IP)

Right now, whatever code, UI/UX designs, or branding we create belongs to us as individuals. In a C-Corp, we all sign an **Invention Assignment Agreement**. This legally transfers all of our app-related work into the corporate vault. If an investor wants to fund us, they need to see that the *company* owns 100% of the software, not three individual guys.

### Clean Equity Splitting and "The Founder Vesting Safety Net"

If we split an LLC 33/33/33 on day one and one person decides to walk away after two months to take a different job, they permanently keep their 33% of our hard work. A C-Corp solves this through a **Stock Vesting Schedule** (the industry standard is a 4-year vest with a 1-year cliff).

* We are awarded our stock on day one, but we don't fully *own* it yet.
* If someone quits before the 1-year mark (the cliff), they leave with **0%** of their stock.
* After year one, the stock unfreezes month by month. This protects the founders who stay and keep building.

## 3. Responsibilities We Need to Figure Out as a Group

Before we hit "submit" on an incorporation platform like *Stripe Atlas* or *Clerky*, the three of us need to sit down and explicitly agree on three major pillars.

### Responsibility Pillar 1: The Equity & Role Split

We need to determine the exact distribution of the company’s stock. While a perfect three-way split (33.3% each) sounds fair, we should base it on responsibilities, time commitments, and who is building the core technology versus managing operations/marketing.

We will authorize a standard bucket of **10,000,000 total shares**. A typical split looks like this:

* **70% to 80% (7,000,000 to 8,000,000 shares):** Divided among the three of us based on our agreed roles.
* **10% to 20% (1,000,000 to 2,000,000 shares):** Set aside in an unissued "Option Pool" to give to our first software engineers, advisors, or community managers down the road.

### Responsibility Pillar 2: Corporate Governance (Who is in Charge?)

A corporation has a strict legal hierarchy. Day-to-day operations don't change, but on paper, we must assign these roles to satisfy state law:

* **The Board of Directors:** The supreme rulers of the company who vote on major decisions (like raising money or selling the company). The three of us will comprise the entire initial Board, meaning we must vote together on major milestones.
* **The Officers:** The individuals authorized to sign legal contracts, open bank accounts, and handle daily operations. We need to officially name a **CEO (Chief Executive Officer)** and a **Secretary** (required by law, can be held by one of the other founders).

### Responsibility Pillar 3: Post-Incorporation Tax Deadlines (The Part That Can Hurt Us)

Once the company is live, we share a collective legal obligation to keep it healthy. There are two non-negotiable tax realities we must handle:

* **The 83(b) Election:** This is a critical IRS tax form. When we get our vesting stock, the IRS will try to tax us every year as the stock "unfreezes" and goes up in value. Filing an 83(b) election tells the IRS, *"Tax me on the value of my stock today ($0), not its future value."* **We have exactly 30 days from the day we incorporate to physically mail this form to the IRS.** If one of us forgets, it creates a massive personal tax bill later.
* **Delaware Franchise Tax & Annual Report:** Every year by March 1st, we must file an annual report and pay a Franchise Tax to Delaware to keep our corporation active. Even if our app makes $0 in its first year, the minimum tax is roughly $175 to $400 depending on the accounting method we choose. We need to ensure we have a shared pool of capital to cover these basic operational costs.
